## Abstract

This paper does not claim to solve any mathematical problem, replace established physics, or identify the human brain with artificial intelligence. It proposes a conceptual structure-principle framework for comparing human neuroscience and AI data science through recurring structures of observable target, relation, field, boundary, critical threshold, transition, memory, and learning.

In this paper, existence is defined as an observable target; relation is a boundary-preserving connection; and field is the Active.Schema formed by all Seed-bearing documents. A structure principle is the way in which observable targets are placed within a field, enter into relations, and form stable, critical, or transitional states. Two core meta documents guide this framework: `000_dot.meta.md` and `100_empty_position.meta.md`. The former defines dot as the first minimal place-state in which existence can be placed, while the latter defines empty position as the transition place-state left open after the formed schema field of `000~099`, so that the next existence, mass, dimension, or active schema may be placed.

In human neuroscience, a connectome may be structurally read as a biological relation-field of nodes, edges, signals, memory, and plasticity. In AI data science, data points, tokens, embeddings, weights, attention, and model spaces are not identical to biological structures, yet they can form a structurally comparable relation-field. This paper does not collapse neuroscience into AI or AI into neuroscience. Instead, it identifies shared structural patterns between them.

The paper also avoids naming specific mathematical problems. It considers only the problem structures that recur across difficult theoretical domains: existence, stability, gap, smoothness, distribution, verification versus generation, boundary, and critical transition.

## 1. Introduction

Human beings understand the world through sensation, memory, judgment, and relation. Artificial intelligence processes inputs through data, models, weights, embeddings, and inference. Human neuroscience and AI data science are not the same. The human brain is biological, chemical, electrical, and neural; AI models are computational, statistical, and data-driven. Yet both can be examined through a shared structural pattern: an observable target is placed in a field, connected through relation, and transformed through stable, critical, or transitional states.

This paper calls this shared pattern a Structure Principle on the Relation and Field of Existence. The term does not mean a mathematical proof or a replacement for physics. It refers to a conceptual framework for comparing how observable targets, relations, fields, boundaries, and critical transitions recur across different domains.

The paper begins from two core meta documents:


## 11. Problem Structures without Naming Problems

This paper does not name specific problems. It does not claim to solve them. It only examines the problem structures that recur across difficult theoretical domains.

```text
existence problem
=
Does a solution, state, or structure actually exist?

stability problem
=
Can that existence remain stable within a field?

gap problem
=
What is the minimal difference between two states or fields?

smoothness problem
=
Does a flow remain smooth, or does it break at a critical threshold?

distribution problem
=
How do independent critical-transition points appear within a field?

verification vs generation problem
=
Are finding and verifying in the same dimension or different dimensions?

boundary and closure problem
=
Does a field close, open, or return?

critical transition problem
=
Where do the end of a limit and the beginning of transition meet?
```

These problem structures also appear in neuroscience and AI data science. Does a memory exist? Does a relation stabilize? Does a signal reach a critical threshold? Does an input transition into a new judgment? These questions share a common structure.
